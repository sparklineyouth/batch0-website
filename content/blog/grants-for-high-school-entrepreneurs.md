**Grants for high school entrepreneurs are pools of free money you apply for and, if chosen, never pay back and never give up ownership for — mostly from pitch competitions, youth business nonprofits, corporate youth programs, and local small-business or community foundations.**

A grant is different from an investment. An investor takes a slice of your company (equity) or expects repayment. A grant is a gift — you usually just spend it on your business and sometimes report how it went. For a teenager with a school schedule, a part-time budget, and no rich uncle, grants are one of the only ways to get real dollars into your startup without signing anything scary.

This is the playbook: where the money lives, who qualifies, what it funds, and how to write an application that beats the pile sitting next to yours.

## Where does the free money actually come from?

There's no single "high school grant" website that lists everything. The money is scattered across four buckets, and you find it by knowing the buckets exist.

| Source type | What it looks like | Typical award | Who qualifies |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pitch competitions | You pitch on a stage or over video; judges pick winners | $250–$10,000 | Often ages 13–18, sometimes a team |
| Youth business nonprofits | Organizations built to fund young founders | $500–$5,000 | Students building a real venture |
| Corporate youth programs | Big companies fund young people in their field | $1,000–$25,000+ | Usually tied to a theme (STEM, climate, food) |
| Local & community foundations | Your city, library, chamber of commerce, credit union | $100–$2,500 | Local residents, sometimes any age |

Notice the pattern: the biggest, most famous national grants have the most applicants and the lowest odds. The small local ones — the credit union that gives out a $500 "young business owner" award nobody's heard of — have maybe a dozen applicants. Chase both, but don't skip the local ones just because the number is smaller. A $500 grant you actually win beats a $10,000 grant you have a 0.5% shot at.

## What kinds of grants can a high schooler realistically win?

You can't win most "small business grants" you'll find by Googling — those require an EIN, a registered business, and often years of tax returns. Skip them. Here's what's genuinely open to a 16-year-old:

- **Pitch and business-plan competitions for students.** These are the most reliable path to real money. The prize is technically a grant. Many are free to enter. Start with our roundup of the [best startup competitions for high school students](/blog/best-startup-competitions-for-high-school-students) and the [business plan competition checklist](/blog/business-plan-competition-checklist).
- **Youth entrepreneurship grants.** Nonprofits exist specifically to hand money to young founders. Search "[your state] youth entrepreneurship grant" and "high school startup grant [current year]."
- **Cause-based grants.** If your startup solves a specific problem — climate, mental health, food access, education — there's often a foundation that funds exactly that, and being young makes your story stronger.
- **School and district innovation funds.** Your school, PTA, or district sometimes has a tiny "student venture" fund. Ask a teacher. Most students never do.
- **Startup credits, not cash.** Not a grant, but free — hosting, software, and tools worth hundreds of dollars. See [how to get free startup credits and perks](/blog/how-to-get-startup-credits-and-perks). Every dollar of credits is a dollar you don't have to raise.

If grants dry up, there are [other ways to fund a high school startup without investors](/blog/how-to-fund-a-high-school-startup) — and you need less money than you think, as [how much money you actually need to start](/blog/how-much-money-to-start-a-business-in-high-school) explains.

## How to find grants nobody else is applying to

The famous grants are famous, so everyone applies. Your edge is finding the quiet ones. Do this in one focused hour:

1. **Search like a local, not a founder.** Try "youth business grant [your city]," "student entrepreneur award [your county]," and "[your state] high school pitch competition." Local results are where your odds are highest.
2. **Check your community's institutions.** Community foundations, credit unions, the chamber of commerce, and big libraries run small grant programs. Their sites are ugly and their programs are under-applied.
3. **Follow the cause, not the money.** Building something for, say, reducing food waste? Search "food waste grant student" or "youth climate grant." Cause-based funders love a young founder.
4. **Ask a teacher or counselor.** They get these emails constantly and forward none of them. Ask directly: "Do you know any grants or competitions for students starting a company?"
5. **Set a Google Alert** for "high school startup grant" and "youth entrepreneur competition [current year]" so deadlines come to you.
6. **Read the eligibility line first.** Confirm your age, location, and stage qualify before you write. Half of "rejections" are just people who applied to the wrong thing.

Keep a simple spreadsheet: grant name, amount, deadline, link, status. You'll apply to more of them, and applying to more of them is the whole game.

## What makes a grant application actually win

Judges read dozens of applications that all say the same vague thing. Yours wins by being specific and by being clearly real. Here's what separates the funded from the ignored:

- **Show it's real, not just an idea.** Reviewers want proof you'll use the money, not disappear. Even 15 [customer interviews](/blog/customer-interviews-beginners-guide) or a 40-person waitlist beats a beautiful idea with zero evidence. If you haven't validated yet, start with [how to validate a startup idea in high school](/blog/how-to-validate-startup-idea-high-school).
- **Answer the exact question they asked.** If the prompt says "What problem are you solving?" write about the problem — not your feature list. Reviewers score against a rubric; give them the words it's looking for.
- **Say exactly where the money goes.** "I'll use the $1,000 for inventory ($400), ads to my first 200 customers ($350), and my LLC and domain ($250)." Specific budgets read as trustworthy. Vague ones read as a wish.
- **Own that you're young.** A high schooler running a real venture is the story. It makes you memorable and reads as resourceful, not risky.
- **Point to real traction.** Even with no revenue, you have something — learn [what to put on a traction slide when you have no revenue](/blog/traction-slide-with-no-revenue).

If the grant comes with a pitch — most do — that's a separate skill worth practicing. Study [how to write a pitch deck for a high school competition](/blog/how-to-write-pitch-deck-high-school-competition) and, when the prize is on the line, [how to actually win the prize money at a pitch competition](/blog/how-to-win-a-pitch-competition-money).

## A quick example so this isn't abstract

Say you're 16, you built a tutoring-matching site for your district with no-code tools, and you have 30 students on a waitlist. You've got $0. You spend one hour and find three grants: a national student pitch competition (long odds, $5,000), your community foundation's "young innovator" micro-grant ($750, maybe 20 applicants), and a local credit union award for student business owners ($500). You apply to all three.

For the community foundation, you write: "This grant will pay for a year of hosting and a paid scheduler plugin ($300), $350 of targeted ads to parents in three feeder schools, and a $100 domain and setup. My waitlist of 30 students shows demand; this money turns it into paying users." You attach one line on how you validated and a screenshot of the waitlist.

That application beats most of the pile — not because your startup is the biggest, but because it's specific, real, and easy to say yes to.

## Do this today

Don't overthink it. Grants reward the people who actually apply, and most people never do.

1. Open a spreadsheet with columns: grant, amount, deadline, link, status.
2. Spend one focused hour running the searches above; add every grant that fits.
3. Apply to the two closest deadlines this week — even a rough draft beats no submission.
4. Reuse the same core answers (problem, traction, budget) across applications so each new one takes 30 minutes, not three hours.
